Using dirty fuels has devastating effects on health. A detriment especially the already most vulnerable communities and individuals can’t opt out of when they want to meet their most basic needs: heating, cooking and lighting.

Immediately the image of a vicious circle comes to mind, when thinking about the consequences of respiratory health issues dramatically diminishing the possibilities to participate in society and economy.

The energy ladder is a model for the idea that with growing socio-economic status, people can (and will) choose cleaner fuels to satisfy their energy needs. Primitive fuels like firewood are substituted by transitioning fuels like kerosene and coal to finally reach the top of the ladder: LPG, electricity and biofuels.

I want to use this model but change the perspective: Can higher access to clean fuels help reducing poverty?

How can we look at this from a data perspective?

Poverty is a complex societal issue, so there are a multitude of perspectives to look at and ways to measure it.

A first one is to look at a single and uniform indicator. The World Bank introduced a threshold of 1.25 int. $ PPP a day. It was updated in 2015 to 1.90 a day. – This measure offers the opportunity to directly juxtapose it with the development of access to clean fuels in a single graph.

Another method would be to look at the context of a country, the state of its development and its own national poverty line to try and offer perspectives on how different changes over time could be related.

We’ll start with this.

Country Spotlight
Ghana

Ghana is not only a good example to show the relationship between poverty and access to clean fuels, it’s efforts and those of NGOs and the private sector are also well documented. In it’s 2012 Sustainable Energy for All Action Plan1 Ghana sets ambitious goals, soon to be re-evaluated.

The plan’s approach to lessen the burden of dirty fuels is twofold: Promoting the use of LPG as a cleaner burning fuel and reducing the consumption of dirty fuels with more efficient cookstoves.

The promotion of efficient cookstoves faces challenges in Ghana: incentives to switch are low for a large group of rural households because they can obtain firewood at minimal or no cost. And metal stoves that don’t allow to prepare the traditional staple foods are not adopted or quickly abandoned. Fortunately there are also success stories: Enterprise Works Ghana for example has sold over 480,000 stoves between 2002 and 2011 helping households to reduce their fuel consumption by up to 40 %.

In 1989, the Ministry of Energy started to promote the use of LPG to targeted households, small-scale businesses and public institutions. LPG cylinders were distributed freely and consumers only had to pay for the gas. These efforts proved to be largely successful. LPG consumption grew from 5,000 tonnes in 1990 to 220,000 in 2009. Further growth was then challenged by Ghana’s dependency on Tema Oil Refinery, it's only national provider for LPG not being able to satisfy the new demand and the fact that commercial transportation started to make use of subsidized LPG to fuel their vehicles.

While we saw access to clean fuels doubling between 2005 and 2015 and a poverty reduction of almost 50 % from 1998 to 2012, universal access to clean fuels is still an urgent issue: In 2012, exposure to indoor air pollution was “responsible for the annual loss of 502,000 disability adjusted life-years”2, resulting in an estimated 16,600 deaths per year in Ghana.

This direct comparison between access to clean fuels and cooking technologies and poverty suggests a quite strong relationship between the two in negative direction.

With occasional outliers most countries show a reduction in poverty as access to clean fuels increases. But these findings also have their limits.

While the relationship is quite obvious for countries where access to clean fuels is above 4 % of the population other factors might be more influential below this threshold and should be researched further.

Nigeria, Chad and Gambia show no relation between the indicators and might be a good comparison to countries that, given the examined timeframe, started just above this threshold – Burkina Faso, Kenya and Ghana.

Conditions vary a lot throughout the African continent and concrete policies must take in consideration how challenges are best met given the specific national and local prerequisites.

Although causation can’t be proven in this context. The found relationship is good news for both SDGs, indicating that improving one of them helps improving both.

What’s at stake? – Dirty Fuels’ Immense Threat to Health
Death Rates from Household Air Pollution

Air pollution death rates (indoor, solid fuels) per 100,00 people in 2015

With 2.6 million deaths from respiratory illness due to household air pollution in 2016, exceeding by far the number of deaths caused by malaria and HIV combined, expanding access to clean fuels is as pressing as important.

SDG 7 sets the goal of universal to ensure universal access to affordable, reliable and modern energy services. While current estimates show a positive trend, it’s not sufficient to reach that goal. Efforts should be intensified to come as close to the 2030’s goal as possible.

Not only would this reduce the burden of respiratory illnesses – as we have seen, there’s also a strong relationship and a positive effect on poverty, increasing the opportunities for the most vulnerable to participate in economy and society.

2030:
Universal access to clean fuels and cooking technologies – leave no one behind!

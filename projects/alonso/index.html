Access to Electricity, Clean Cooking Technologies and Under Five Mortality Rate in Africa
According to the WHO, Africa remains the region were the risk of dying before completing five years of age remains the highest in the world. The Continent is home to five of the six countries with an under five mortality rate above 100 deaths per 1000 live births. As part of the SDG agenda, the UN has set the goal of reducing the under five mortality rate to at least as low as 25 per 1000 live births. The following project explores the relationship between access to electricity, clean cooking technologies, under five morality rate in Africa and how have they have changed over time. As a final element we take a closer look at Nigeria in order to evaluate some of the consequences of having unequal access to electricity and clean cooking technologies at a more granular level.
Access to Electricity and Under Five Mortality Rate in Africa
Access to electricity can significantly improve the wellbeing of the population. Counting on reliable sources of energy can act as a critical enabler for the delivery of health services as it enhances the conditions of hospitals and clinics throughout the Continent. Trustworthy electricity systems secure better lighting conditions in emergency care rooms, small clinics can keep the vaccines and medicines refrigerated, hospitals can perform studies that require scans without any interruptions. In the households, electricity can help preserve the food, heat the water and expand the food preparation process. This actions can reduce the risk of getting infected.
Access to Clean Cooking Technologies and Under Five Mortality Rate in Africa
Improving access to clean cooking technologies in Africa is one of the most important goals of the Continent. Unreliable power supplies prompt families to increase the usage of indoor fires and kerosene lamps causing indoor pollution in many households across the Continent. Relying on these technologies heavily increase the probability of contracting acute lower respiratory infections. Children under five are the most vulnerable group of the population that gets constantly exposed to the risks of using inefficient and dangerous fuels. The problem is particularly grave in Sub-Saharan Africa, where, according to a recent report elaborated by the World Bank, over 82% of the population (~700 million people) remains dependent on solid fuels such as charcoal, dung, fuelwood, and other biomass for cooking purposes.
Case Study: Factors that Contribute to Under Five Mortality Rate in Nigeria
Under five morality rate Is caused by several factors. In Africa, access to electricity and clean cooking technologies are vital in order to save thousands of lives. Although the number of children dying before the age of five has been significantly reduced, a lot of work needs to be done in order to reduce the risks that may cause more casualties. Nigeria remains one of the countries with the highest rates of under five mortality in the Continent. Even though Nigeria has generally improved the access to electricity of its population, the energy sources remain highly unreliable. The World Bank lists Nigeria as one of three countries experiencing more than 60 power outages every week, along with the Comoros and Guinea. This can cause the malfunctioning of hospital equipment and it hinders the capabilities of small clinics near rural areas. This is critical for a country where 76.9% of the deliveries occur in rural households at a time where consistent access to electricity is fundamental in order to guarantee a healthy and clean environment for the newborns. On the other hand, cooking with dirty fuels is a common practice in Nigerian households and it is very common for the least favored population to develop acute respiratory infections (ARI).
